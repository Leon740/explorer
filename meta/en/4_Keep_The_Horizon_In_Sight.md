# Keep the Horizon in Sight

> **Closed doors are not the problem.**
>
> **The real problem is losing sight of the horizon.**

## Problem #1. Complete Immersion

Ambitious people rarely set easy goals for themselves. Their goals are usually so significant that they demand complete commitment. A great goal requires complete focus. That is why people become so deeply immersed in the process that they stop seeing everything else around them. This is normal. Without this level of immersion, it's almost impossible to achieve anything remarkable. But this is also where the first danger appears: during the daily struggle, people gradually lose sight of the horizon. They begin living only for today's task.

> The means become more important than the goal itself.

## Problem #2. Decomposition

A great goal is too abstract to pursue directly. It has to be broken down into multiple levels.

For example:

```text
Be happy.
↓
Realize my full potential.
↓
Do work I genuinely enjoy.
↓
Become an exceptional engineer.
↓
Work at an innovative company.
↓
Prepare for the interview.
↓
Solve today's task.
```

Each level makes the goal more concrete. Without this decomposition, it's impossible to make meaningful progress every day. But another problem appears:

> The deeper you move down the goal tree, the easier it becomes to forget why you started in the first place.

## Problem #3. Losing — and Changing — Your Course

Sometimes a person becomes so invested in one local objective that it starts feeling like the ultimate goal. For example, the original goal might have been to become healthy, strong, and energetic. Then comes swimming: competitions, personal records, better results. Eventually it begins to feel as though becoming a great swimmer was always the goal. It wasn't. It was only one possible implementation.

But there's another possibility. As you move through life, you may realize that what changed wasn't the implementation — it was the goal itself. Perhaps you once dreamed of working for a specific company. Years later, you discover that teaching, sports, or building your own business brings you far more fulfillment.

> This isn't losing your way. It's a deeper understanding of yourself.

## Problem #4. Ressentiment

When the journey becomes harder than expected, another danger appears. Instead of looking for a different path, people begin convincing themselves that the goal was never worth pursuing.

"I didn't want it anyway."
"That company is overrated."
"That relationship wasn't important."
"That sport isn't even interesting."

This is called **ressentiment**—a psychological defense mechanism where being unable to obtain something gradually turns into denying its value. It feels like relief. In reality, it often hides disappointment.

> It's important to distinguish an honest change of goals from an attempt to protect your ego. Sometimes your values genuinely change. Sometimes only the story you tell yourself changes.

## Pause

Sometimes you need to stop. Not to rest. But to see the horizon again. Stepping away from day-to-day execution allows you to see the entire picture.

Ask yourself:

- What am I doing right now?
- Why am I doing it?
- Is this bringing me closer to my real goal?

A useful tool here is the **Five Whys** technique: for every answer, simply ask:

> Why?

For example:

```text
I want to work at a particular company.
↓
Why?
↓
Because it has a strong engineering culture.
↓
Why does that matter?
↓
Because I want to become an exceptional engineer.
↓
Why?
↓
Because I want to realize my full potential.
↓
Why?
↓
Because that's what gives my life meaning and fulfillment.
```

> After only a few questions, it becomes clear that the original goal was merely one possible way of reaching a much deeper one.

## Closed Doors

Closed doors are inevitable. You won't get every job. Not every attempt will succeed.

> But a closed door doesn't test your goal. It only tests your chosen path. If the goal truly matters, there is almost always another route.

## Constraints

Constraints rarely prevent you from reaching your goal. More often, they reveal the difference between the goal and the method you chose to achieve it. An injury forces you to switch sports. A rejection forces you to look for another company. Failure forces you to rethink your strategy.

> Constraints are what make people stronger.

Strong people are not shaped because life was easy. They are shaped because obstacles forced them, again and again, to find a new path toward the same horizon.

## Conclusion

Methods change. Plans change. Circumstances change. Sometimes even the goals themselves change.

> But as long as you regularly lift your eyes back to the horizon, you can always adjust your course.

The real danger isn't closed doors. The real danger is becoming so consumed by the struggle that you forget why you started fighting in the first place.

Keep the horizon in sight. It is the horizon that gives direction. The wind will change. The route will change. The plan will change.

> But as long as you don't lose sight of the horizon, you'll always be able to find another way.
